## Working Backwards

If we know the resulting confidence interval from a sample, then we can work backwards to find the sample mean $\overline x$ and margin of error $E$ for the sample.

The sample $\overline x$ is in the center (or middle) of the confidence interval, therefore, to calculate $\overline x$ use the formula

$$\overline x=\frac{\mathrm{Upper~Limit~+~Lower~Limit}}{2}$$

$$=\frac{\hat{\theta}_{U}+\hat{\theta}_{L}}{2}$$

The margin of error is half the width of the confidence interval, therefore, to calculate $E$ use the formula

$$E=\frac{\mathrm{Upper~Limit~-~Lower~Limit}}{2}$$

$$=\frac{\hat{\theta}_{U}+\hat{\theta}_{L}}{2}$$

Both of these formulas are to be used when you know the resulting confidence interval from a study, but other information is unknown.

### Example

**Problem:** A confidence interval for the population mean, $\mu$, is $12.1<\mu<12.9$. Find $\overline x$ and $E$ from this confidence interval.

**Solution:**
$\overline x=\frac{\hat{\theta}_{U}+\hat{\theta}_{L}}{2}=\frac{12.9+12.1}{2}=12.5$

$E=\frac{\hat{\theta}_{U}+\hat{\theta}_{L}}{2}=\frac{12.9-12.1}{2}=0.4$

Thus, we can rewrite the confidence interval as $12.5 \pm 0.4$
